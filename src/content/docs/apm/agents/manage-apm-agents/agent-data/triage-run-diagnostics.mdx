Once you've installed a New Relic <InlinePopover type="apm"/> agent, the APM summary page is your operational hub. It's where you go to understand whether your service is healthy, investigate degradation, and verify that a recent deployment didn't introduce problems.

To open it, go to <DNT>**[one.newrelic.com > All capabilities](https://one.newrelic.com/all-capabilities) > APM & services**</DNT> and select your application.

This page covers three situations you'll face regularly:

1. [Establishing a baseline after install](#baseline) — understand what normal looks like before you need to triage anything
2. [Triaging when something goes wrong](#triage) — a customer complained or an alert fired; work through the page top to bottom
3. [Validating a deployment](#deployment) — you just shipped code and want to know if you broke anything

## Establish a baseline after install [#baseline]

The first time you open the APM summary page, your goal isn't to find problems — it's to learn what normal looks like for your service. Without a baseline, you can't tell the difference between a bad day and a genuine incident.

### Read your Apdex score

<img
  title="apdex in APM"
  alt="A screenshot depicting a sample Apdex score in APM"
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_apdex-in-apm.webp"
/>

Start with Apdex. It's a single score from 0 to 1 that represents how satisfied users are with your service's response time — the closer to 1, the better.

On a fresh install, your score gives you an immediate read on whether your service is performing acceptably:

* **0.85–0.95:** Healthy. Your Apdex T threshold is well-tuned to your service.
* **0.7–0.85:** Some friction. Worth watching but not urgent.
* **Below 0.7:** Users are experiencing degraded service. Investigate now.
* **Above 0.95:** Your Apdex T may be set too generously. Consider lowering it in **Settings** so the score reflects reality.

<Callout variant="tip">
  The default Apdex T is 0.5 seconds. If your service is inherently slower (a background job, for example) or faster (a lightweight API), adjust the threshold under **Settings** so the score reflects your service's actual expectations — not a generic default.
</Callout>

For more, see [Apdex: measure user satisfaction](/docs/apm/new-relic-apm/apdex/apdex-measure-user-satisfaction/).

### Learn your traffic pattern

<img
  title="throughput in APM"
  alt="A screenshot depicting throughput in APM."
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-full_throughput-in-apm.webp"
/>

The **Throughput** chart shows requests per minute (rpm). Observe it across different times of day to understand your traffic rhythm: when is peak load? When is it quietest?

This matters because other metrics need context. A 5% error rate during a traffic spike tells a different story than the same rate at 3am. Knowing your normal traffic pattern lets you interpret future anomalies accurately.

### Note how response time is distributed

<img
  title="web transactions apm summary"
  alt="A screenshot depicting web transactions in the APM summary page"
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_web-transactions-in-APM.webp"
/>

The **Web transactions** chart breaks response time into three segments:

* **Blue:** all transactions
* **Yellow:** database operations
* **Green:** external service calls

Note the proportion of each segment during normal operation. If database time typically accounts for 20% of your response time and that segment later jumps to 60%, you know immediately where to look during a future incident — before even digging into transactions.

### Set up alerts before you need them

<Callout variant="tip">
  On a fresh install, some summary tiles will appear empty. Each tile requires its own feature to be configured before it shows data. Click **Set up now** on any empty tile to get started.
</Callout>

The **Issues** tile only surfaces data if you have alert conditions configured for this entity. An empty tile means you won't know when your service degrades unless you're actively watching dashboards.

Configure alert conditions for error rate, Apdex, and throughput while your service is healthy. That way, thresholds are set against your real baseline — not guesswork. See [Set up your first alert condition](/docs/alerts-applied-intelligence/new-relic-alerts/get-started/your-first-nrql-condition/).

## Triage when something goes wrong [#triage]

A customer reported slowness. An alert fired. The APM summary page lets you quickly narrow down where to look without jumping between tools. Work through it top to bottom.

### Step 1: Check the summary tiles

<img
  title="apm summary tiles"
  alt="A screenshot depicting the apm summary tiles"
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_apm-summary-tiles.webp"
/>

The four tiles at the top of the page are your fast-triage layer. Before diving into charts, scan these first:

* **Issues:** Are there open alert incidents? If multiple conditions fired simultaneously, the problem is likely widespread — not a single bad transaction.
* **Last deployment:** Did someone ship code recently? A deployment 30 minutes before complaints started is a strong lead. If this tile shows a spike in error rate or response time after a release, jump ahead to [validating a deployment](#deployment).
* **Service levels:** Is your error budget being burned faster than expected? This tells you whether the degradation is customer-impacting at scale, not just a few isolated errors.
* **Vulnerabilities:** Less relevant for performance issues, but worth a glance if the behavior is unusual or unexpected.

### Step 2: Identify which metric is the signal

Next, look at the four key metric charts to understand what type of problem you're dealing with.

<CollapserGroup>
  <Collapser
    id="apdex-drop"
    title="Apdex dropped"
  >
    <img
      title="apdex in APM"
      alt="A screenshot depicting a sample Apdex score in APM"
      src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_apdex-in-apm.webp"
    />

    A falling Apdex score means users are experiencing slower or failed responses. Check **Web transactions** next to see whether response time increased across all transactions or only in specific segments.

    If the database segment (yellow) spiked, the problem likely lives in your queries or database host. If the external segment (green) spiked, a downstream dependency may be the culprit.

    <Callout variant="tip">
      An Apdex score of 0 means every request is erroring. Go to the **Errors** chart immediately — every error automatically scores 0 on Apdex.
    </Callout>
  </Collapser>

  <Collapser
    id="error-spike"
    title="Error rate spiked"
  >
    <img
      title="errors in APM"
      alt="A screenshot showing errors in APM."
      src="/images/apm_screenshot-full_errors-in-APM.webp"
    />

    The **Errors** chart shows the percentage of transactions that resulted in an error (`TransactionError` and `ErrorTrace` events).

    A spike in errors without a corresponding spike in throughput suggests the cause is a code change or a failing dependency — not just traffic overload. Check the **Last deployment** tile to see if a recent release correlates with the spike onset.

    Use the dropdown to switch to **Impacted users** view to understand the customer impact before deciding how urgently to respond. For next steps, see [Manage errors](/docs/apm/agents/manage-apm-agents/agent-data/manage-errors-apm-collect-ignore-or-mark-expected/).
  </Collapser>

  <Collapser
    id="throughput-change"
    title="Throughput changed unexpectedly"
  >
    <img
      title="throughput in APM"
      alt="A screenshot depicting throughput in APM."
      src="/images/apm_screenshot-full_throughput-in-apm.webp"
    />

    **Higher than usual throughput** during a period of degradation suggests your service is overwhelmed — it's receiving more requests than it can handle. Check the **Infrastructure** section to see whether CPU or memory is saturated on any host.

    **Lower than usual throughput** — especially if it's not an off-peak hour and no deployments went out — may mean an upstream service that calls yours is broken, so fewer requests are getting through. Check **Distributed tracing insights** to look for upstream signal.
  </Collapser>
</CollapserGroup>

### Step 3: Rule out upstream and downstream causes

<img
  title="distributed tracing in APM"
  alt="A screenshot depicting distributed tracing information on the APM summary page"
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_distributed-tracing-in-the-apm-summary-page.webp"
/>

Before concluding the problem lives in your service, check **Distributed tracing insights**. This section surfaces upstream and downstream entities that are showing correlated changes in response time, error rate, or throughput at the same time as your service.

If a downstream service your code calls is showing increased latency, the problem may not be yours to fix. Click **View trace** to see the full trace and identify exactly where time is being introduced.

Learn more in the [distributed tracing insights docs](/docs/distributed-tracing/ui-data/related-trace-entity-signals/).

### Step 4: Check whether it's one host or all of them

<img
  title="Infrastructure summary page"
  alt="A screenshot depicting the infrastructure section of the apm summary page."
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_infrastructure-on-apm-summary-page.webp"
/>

Scroll to the **Infrastructure** section and look at the per-host table. Compare response time, throughput, error rate, CPU, and memory across hosts.

* **One host stands out:** The problem is likely isolated — a bad deployment that landed on one instance, a resource constraint, or a configuration drift on that host.
* **All hosts show the same pattern:** The cause is shared — a database, an external API, or a code change that hit every instance equally.

This distinction saves significant investigation time. A single-host problem calls for a different response than a fleet-wide one.

### Step 5: Look for log patterns

<img
  title="logs in apm"
  alt="A screenshot showing logs in apm"
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_logs-in-apm.webp"
/>

The **Logs** chart gives you a summary view of your application logs through the [logs in context](/docs/logs/logs-context/get-started-logs-context/) feature.

Select **Log patterns (top 10)** to surface groups of logs that share the same structure. A new pattern that appeared around the time of the incident — one you don't recognize from your baseline — is worth investigating first. Click **Logs** to go to the full logs UI for deeper analysis.

For more about log patterns, see [Log patterns](/docs/logs/ui-data/find-unusual-logs-log-patterns/).

## Validate a deployment [#deployment]

After shipping code, use the APM summary page as a quick health check. The goal is to catch regressions early — before customers report them.

### Check the deployment tile first

<img
  title="deployment summary tile"
  alt="A screenshot showing the deployment summary tile"
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_deployment-summary-tile.webp"
/>

The **Last deployment** tile automatically compares your service's behavior before and after your most recent deployment marker, showing the change in error rate and response time without any manual setup.

The comparison window grows proportionally: if you deployed 45 minutes ago, the tile compares the 45 minutes before and after. After three hours, it settles into a standard 3-hour comparison window.

<Callout variant="tip">
  This tile only populates if you've set up [deployment markers](/docs/apm/apm-ui-pages/events/record-deployments/). If the tile is empty, you're flying blind after every release. Setting up markers is one of the highest-value instrumentation steps you can take.
</Callout>

### Look for correlated changes across charts

A regression typically shows up as a correlated shift across two charts at the same time. Look for:

* **Error rate rising** in the **Errors** chart
* **Response time increasing** in **Web transactions** — especially in the database (yellow) or external (green) segment if the deployment touched those code paths

Deployment markers appear as gray pins on each chart. If you see both metrics worsen at the same pin, you have a strong signal that the deployment introduced a regression.

If the spike is confined to the database segment, the regression is likely a slow or missing query introduced by the change. If it's in the external segment, check whether the deployment changed how your service calls a downstream API.

### Watch your error budget

If you have [service levels configured](/docs/service-level-management/intro-slm/), the **Service levels** tile will tell you whether the deployment is burning error budget faster than expected. A tile moving to **At risk** shortly after a release warrants a close watch. **Non-compliant** warrants a rollback conversation.
