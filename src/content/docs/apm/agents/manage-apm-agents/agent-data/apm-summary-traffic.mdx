The APM summary page gives you a continuous view of how much traffic your service is handling and how that traffic relates to performance. Use it to understand your service's capacity, spot anomalies before they become incidents, and make informed scaling decisions.

To open the APM summary page, go to <DNT>**[one.newrelic.com > All capabilities](https://one.newrelic.com/all-capabilities) > APM & services**</DNT> and select your application.

## Read the throughput chart [#throughput-chart]

<img
  title="throughput in APM"
  alt="A screenshot depicting throughput in APM."
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-full_throughput-in-apm.webp"
/>

The **Throughput** chart shows requests per minute (rpm) over your selected time range. Each request that your agent instruments counts toward this number — web transactions (HTTP requests), background jobs, and message queue consumers each appear as separate lines if your service handles more than one type.

**What the chart tells you:**

| Pattern | What it means |
|---|---|
| Smooth peak-and-valley rhythm | Normal traffic cycle — your service has predictable load patterns |
| Sudden spike | Traffic burst — check whether it correlates with a marketing event, upstream change, or bot activity |
| Gradual climb with no return | Organic growth, or a memory/connection leak causing requests to queue |
| Sudden drop (not off-peak) | Upstream service may be broken and sending fewer requests; check [distributed tracing insights](/docs/distributed-tracing/ui-data/related-trace-entity-signals/) |

## Compare traffic across time [#time-comparison]

Looking at throughput in isolation doesn't tell you much. Compare it against a reference period to spot deviations.

**What to do:**
1. Open the time picker (top right of the page) and select your current window (for example, last 3 hours).
2. Click **Compare with** and select **Previous period** or a specific date to overlay the two time series on the same chart.
3. Look for divergence — a period where current throughput is higher or lower than the reference line.

<Callout variant="tip">
  Use **Last 7 days** with day-over-day comparison to see your weekly traffic rhythm. Most services have predictable patterns — Mondays differ from Saturdays, morning peaks differ from overnight troughs. Knowing this prevents false alarms during routine low-traffic periods.
</Callout>

## Find your busiest endpoints [#busiest-endpoints]

The throughput chart shows aggregate traffic, but most services have a small number of endpoints that handle the majority of requests. Finding these matters for capacity planning and targeted optimization.

**What to do:**
1. From the APM summary page, click **Transactions** in the left nav.
2. In the **Sort** dropdown, select **Most throughput (rpm)**.
3. The top entries are your highest-traffic endpoints. Note which ones dominate — these are the first to saturate under load and the most impactful targets for performance work.

<Callout variant="tip">
  Cross-reference your highest-throughput endpoints with your slowest endpoints (sort by **Slowest average response time**). An endpoint that is both high-traffic and slow is your highest-leverage optimization target.
</Callout>

## Correlate traffic with performance [#traffic-performance-correlation]

<img
  title="web transactions apm summary"
  alt="A screenshot depicting web transactions in the APM summary page"
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_web-transactions-in-APM.webp"
/>

How your service's response time behaves as traffic increases tells you a lot about its health and capacity headroom.

| What you observe | What it means | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| Throughput rises, response time stays flat | Healthy — service scales well within current load | Set a throughput alert threshold just above your observed peak to catch future overload early |
| Throughput rises, response time rises proportionally | Approaching capacity — the service is doing more work but keeping up | Investigate database connection pool limits and external call concurrency; consider horizontal scaling |
| Throughput is normal, response time spikes | Not a traffic problem — a dependency degraded or a code path slowed | Use [triage workflow](/docs/apm/agents/manage-apm-agents/agent-data/apm-summary-triage) to find the cause |
| Throughput drops, response time also drops | Traffic naturally decreased (off-peak) | Normal — no action needed |
| Throughput drops, response time spikes | Upstream caller is broken or slow; the requests getting through are the expensive ones | Check distributed tracing insights for upstream signal |

## Set traffic-based alerts [#traffic-alerts]

Traffic anomalies — both spikes and drops — often precede or indicate incidents. Alerting on throughput directly gives you early warning.

**What to do:**
1. Go to <DNT>**[one.newrelic.com > Alerts](https://one.newrelic.com/alerts)**</DNT> and create a new alert condition.
2. Use NRQL: `SELECT rate(count(*), 1 minute) FROM Transaction WHERE appName = 'your-app-name'`
3. Set thresholds based on your observed peak and trough:
   - **Upper threshold:** 20–30% above your typical peak rpm — catches traffic spikes before they saturate the service.
   - **Lower threshold:** 20–30% below your typical off-peak trough — catches upstream failures that starve your service of requests.

<Callout variant="tip">
  Avoid setting round-number thresholds like "alert if throughput exceeds 1000 rpm." Tune against your actual observed peak, not an arbitrary number — otherwise you'll get false positives on normal heavy-traffic days or miss incidents because your service's normal peak is higher than expected.
</Callout>

For more on building NRQL alert conditions, see [Create your first NRQL alert condition](/docs/alerts-applied-intelligence/new-relic-alerts/get-started/your-first-nrql-condition/).
