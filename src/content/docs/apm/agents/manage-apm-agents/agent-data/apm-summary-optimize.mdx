Not every performance problem shows up as an incident. Sometimes the service is technically "up" but response times are higher than they should be, Apdex is hovering in an acceptable but not great range, or you know from user feedback that the app feels slow. This page shows you how to use the APM summary page to find what to fix and in what order.

To open the APM summary page, go to <DNT>**[one.newrelic.com > All capabilities](https://one.newrelic.com/all-capabilities) > APM & services**</DNT> and select your application.

## Start with Apdex to size the problem [#apdex-baseline]

<img
  title="apdex in APM"
  alt="A screenshot depicting a sample Apdex score in APM"
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_apdex-in-apm.webp"
/>

Before optimizing anything, establish how much room for improvement actually exists.

| Apdex score | What it means for optimization |
|---|---|
| 0.95–1.0 | Users are satisfied. Optimization is unlikely to produce noticeable user impact. |
| 0.85–0.95 | Some friction exists. Targeted improvements to your slowest endpoints will move this meaningfully. |
| 0.7–0.85 | Users are noticeably affected. Multiple endpoints likely need attention. |
| Below 0.7 | Significant user impact. Treat this as an active problem, not a tuning exercise — use the [triage workflow](/docs/apm/agents/manage-apm-agents/agent-data/apm-summary-triage) instead. |

<Callout variant="tip">
  If Apdex is above 0.95 but users are still complaining, your Apdex T threshold may be too generous. Check **Settings > Application** and lower the threshold to reflect what users actually expect. This will give you a more accurate picture.
</Callout>

## Find where time is being spent [#where-time-goes]

<img
  title="web transactions apm summary"
  alt="A screenshot depicting web transactions in the APM summary page"
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_web-transactions-in-APM.webp"
/>

The **Web transactions** chart breaks total response time into three segments. This tells you which layer to focus your optimization work on before you look at individual transactions.

| Dominant segment | Where to look next | What you're likely to find |
|---|---|---|
| Yellow (database) is largest | **[APM > Databases](/docs/apm/apm-ui-pages/monitoring/databases-page-view-operations-throughput-response-time/)** | Slow queries, missing indexes, N+1 query patterns, excessive query volume |
| Green (external) is largest | **[APM > External services](/docs/apm/apm-ui-pages/monitoring/external-services/external-services-intro/)** | Slow third-party APIs, synchronous calls that could be async, missing caches |
| Blue (app code) is largest with small yellow/green | **[APM > Transactions](/docs/apm/apm-ui-pages/monitoring/transactions-page-find-specific-performance-problems/)** | CPU-heavy computation, inefficient algorithms, excessive object allocation |

<Callout variant="tip">
  If yellow and green together account for more than 60% of total response time, your application code is probably fine — the bottleneck is your dependencies. Optimizing application code in that situation produces minimal user-visible improvement.
</Callout>

## Prioritize which endpoints to fix [#prioritize-endpoints]

Not all slow endpoints are equally worth fixing. Prioritize by multiplying impact (how slow) by frequency (how often it's called).

**What to do:**
1. Go to **Transactions** in the left nav.
2. Sort by **Most time consuming** — this ranks endpoints by total time spent (duration × call count). The top entries are your highest-leverage targets because they combine slowness with high frequency.
3. For each top endpoint, click through to see the transaction trace breakdown. Note which segment (database, external, or application code) dominates.
4. Compare against **Slowest average response time** — this catches endpoints that are extremely slow but called infrequently. These may matter less for aggregate performance but a lot for specific user journeys.

| Sort order | Best for |
|---|---|
| Most time consuming | Maximum aggregate performance improvement |
| Slowest average response time | Fixing the worst user experience on specific pages or operations |
| Most throughput | Finding endpoints where even small improvements multiply across millions of calls |

## Check database query performance [#database-optimization]

Database time is the most common source of avoidable latency in web applications. Even if the yellow segment isn't dominant, it's worth a quick review.

**What to do:**
1. Go to **[APM > Databases](/docs/apm/apm-ui-pages/monitoring/databases-page-view-operations-throughput-response-time/)**.
2. Sort by **Most time consuming**.
3. Look for:
   - **High call count with low individual duration:** Often an N+1 pattern — one request is triggering dozens of small queries that could be batched.
   - **Low call count with high individual duration:** A missing index or a full table scan on a growing dataset.
   - **Any query whose average duration increased over time:** The table is growing and the query isn't scaling with it.

## Validate the impact of changes [#validate-improvements]

After making an optimization, confirm it actually moved the needle before moving on.

**What to do:**
1. Set a [deployment marker](/docs/apm/apm-ui-pages/events/record-deployments/) when you deploy the optimization.
2. Return to the APM summary page after 30–60 minutes.
3. The **Last deployment** tile shows the before/after comparison automatically. Look for:
   - Apdex improvement
   - Response time reduction in the relevant Web transactions segment
   - Throughput holding steady or improving (optimization shouldn't reduce throughput)
4. Go to **Transactions** and confirm the specific endpoint you targeted improved.

<Callout variant="tip">
  If the deployment tile shows no change, the optimization may not have addressed the actual bottleneck. Return to the Web transactions chart and re-examine which segment is still dominant.
</Callout>
