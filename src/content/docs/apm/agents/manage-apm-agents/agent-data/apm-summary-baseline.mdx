The first time you open the APM summary page after installing an agent, your goal isn't to find problems — it's to learn what normal looks like. Without a baseline, you can't tell the difference between a bad day and a genuine incident.

To open the APM summary page, go to <DNT>**[one.newrelic.com > All capabilities](https://one.newrelic.com/all-capabilities) > APM & services**</DNT> and select your application.

<Callout variant="tip">
  On a fresh install, some summary tiles will appear empty. Each tile requires its own feature to be configured before it shows data. Click **Set up now** on any empty tile to get started.
</Callout>

## Read your Apdex score [#apdex]

<img
  title="apdex in APM"
  alt="A screenshot depicting a sample Apdex score in APM"
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_apdex-in-apm.webp"
/>

Apdex is a single score from 0 to 1 that represents how satisfied users are with your service's response time — the closer to 1, the better.

On a fresh install, your score gives you an immediate read on whether your service is performing acceptably:

| Apdex range | What it means |
|---|---|
| 0.85–0.95 | Healthy. Threshold is well-tuned to your service. |
| 0.7–0.85 | Some friction. Worth watching but not urgent. |
| Below 0.7 | Users are experiencing degraded service. Investigate now. |
| Above 0.95 | Apdex T may be set too generously — consider lowering it in **Settings**. |

<Callout variant="tip">
  The default Apdex T is 0.5 seconds. If your service is inherently slower (a background job) or faster (a lightweight API), adjust the threshold under **Settings** so the score reflects your service's actual expectations.
</Callout>

For more, see [Apdex: measure user satisfaction](/docs/apm/new-relic-apm/apdex/apdex-measure-user-satisfaction/).

## Learn your traffic pattern [#traffic]

<img
  title="throughput in APM"
  alt="A screenshot depicting throughput in APM."
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-full_throughput-in-apm.webp"
/>

The **Throughput** chart shows requests per minute (rpm). Observe it across different times of day to understand your traffic rhythm: when is peak load? When is it quietest?

This context matters for future triage. A 5% error rate during a traffic spike tells a different story than the same rate at 3 AM. Knowing your normal traffic pattern lets you interpret anomalies accurately instead of reacting to noise.

For a deeper look at traffic patterns and endpoint breakdown, see [Understand traffic patterns with APM](/docs/apm/agents/manage-apm-agents/agent-data/apm-summary-traffic).

## Note how response time is distributed [#response-time]

<img
  title="web transactions apm summary"
  alt="A screenshot depicting web transactions in the APM summary page"
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_web-transactions-in-APM.webp"
/>

The **Web transactions** chart breaks response time into three segments:

| Segment color | What it represents |
|---|---|
| Blue | All transactions (total response time) |
| Yellow | Database operations |
| Green | External service calls |

Note the proportion of each segment during normal operation. If database time typically accounts for 20% of your response time and that segment later jumps to 60%, you know immediately where to look during a future incident — before even digging into individual transactions.

## Set up alerts before you need them [#alerts]

The **Issues** tile only surfaces data if you have alert conditions configured for this entity. An empty tile means you won't know when your service degrades unless you're actively watching dashboards.

Configure alert conditions for error rate, Apdex, and throughput while your service is healthy. Setting thresholds against your real baseline — not guesswork — means fewer false positives and fewer missed incidents.

See [Set up your first alert condition](/docs/alerts-applied-intelligence/new-relic-alerts/get-started/your-first-nrql-condition/).
