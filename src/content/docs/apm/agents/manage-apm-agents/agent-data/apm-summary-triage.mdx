A customer reported slowness. An alert fired. The APM summary page lets you quickly narrow down where to look without jumping between tools. Work through it top to bottom.

**Start here based on what you're seeing:**

| Symptom | Jump to |
|---|---|
| "The app is slow" | [Apdex dropped](#apdex-drop) |
| "Users are getting errors / 500s" | [Error rate spiked](#error-spike) |
| "Traffic dropped or the app seems down" | [Throughput changed](#throughput-change) |
| "It was fine before the last deploy" | [Step 1: Summary tiles](#step-1) |
| "One server seems to be the problem" | [Step 4: Infrastructure](#step-4) |
| "I don't know where to start" | Start at Step 1 and work down |

To open the APM summary page, go to <DNT>**[one.newrelic.com > All capabilities](https://one.newrelic.com/all-capabilities) > APM & services**</DNT> and select your application.

## Step 1: Check the summary tiles [#step-1]

<img
  title="apm summary tiles"
  alt="A screenshot depicting the apm summary tiles"
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_apm-summary-tiles.webp"
/>

The four tiles at the top of the page are your fast-triage layer. Scan these before diving into charts.

* **Issues:** Are there open alert incidents? Multiple conditions firing simultaneously means the problem is likely widespread — not a single bad transaction.
* **Last deployment:** Did someone ship code recently? A deployment 30 minutes before complaints started is a strong lead. If this tile shows a spike in error rate or response time after a release, go to [Validate deployments](/docs/apm/agents/manage-apm-agents/agent-data/apm-summary-deployment) instead.
* **Service levels:** Is your error budget burning faster than expected? This tells you whether the degradation is customer-impacting at scale.
* **Vulnerabilities:** Less relevant for performance issues, but worth a glance if the behavior is unusual or unexpected.

## Step 2: Identify which metric is the signal [#step-2]

Look at the key metric charts to understand what type of problem you're dealing with.

  <Collapser

    title="Apdex dropped"
  >
    <img
      title="apdex in APM"
      alt="A screenshot depicting a sample Apdex score in APM"
      src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_apdex-in-apm.webp"

    A falling Apdex score means users are experiencing slower or failed responses.

    **What to do:**
    1. Look at the **Web transactions** chart directly below Apdex. It breaks response time into three colored segments.
    2. Find which segment grew at the same time Apdex fell:
       - **Yellow (database) spiked:** Go to **[one.newrelic.com > APM > your app > Databases](/docs/apm/apm-ui-pages/monitoring/databases-page-view-operations-throughput-response-time/)** to find which query slowed down.
       - **Green (external) spiked:** Go to **[one.newrelic.com > APM > your app > External services](/docs/apm/apm-ui-pages/monitoring/external-services/external-services-intro/)** to see which downstream call degraded.
       - **Blue (app code) spiked with no yellow/green change:** Go to **Transactions** and sort by **Most time consuming** to find which endpoint regressed.

    <Callout variant="tip">
      An Apdex score of 0 means every request is erroring. Open the **Error rate spiked** collapser below — every error automatically scores 0 on Apdex.
    </Callout>
  </Collapser>

  <Collapser
    id="error-spike"
    title="Error rate spiked"
  >
    <img
      title="errors in APM"
      alt="A screenshot showing errors in APM."

    The **Errors** chart shows the percentage of transactions that resulted in an error.

    **What to do:**
    1. Use the chart dropdown to switch to **Impacted users** view. Five errors from one internal user warrants a different response than 500 errors affecting paying customers.
    2. Check whether throughput also spiked at the same time. If not, this is a code or dependency failure — not traffic overload.
    3. Check the **Last deployment** tile. A recent deployment correlating with the error onset is a strong rollback signal.
    4. Click **View errors** (or go to **[one.newrelic.com > APM > your app > Errors inbox](/docs/errors-inbox/apm-tab/)**) to see stack traces and identify the specific exception.

    For next steps, see [Manage errors](/docs/apm/agents/manage-apm-agents/agent-data/manage-errors-apm-collect-ignore-or-mark-expected/).
  </Collapser>

  <Collapser
    id="throughput-change"
    title="Throughput changed unexpectedly"
  >
    <img
      title="throughput in APM"
      alt="A screenshot depicting throughput in APM."

    **If throughput is higher than usual** and the service is degrading:
    1. Scroll to the **Infrastructure** section and check CPU and memory across hosts. If a host is pegged at 100% CPU, it's the bottleneck.
    2. If resources look fine, check **Databases** — high throughput can overwhelm a connection pool.

    **If throughput is lower than usual** (outside off-peak hours, no deployments):
    1. Check **Distributed tracing insights** — an upstream service may be broken and sending you fewer requests.
    2. Check your load balancer or upstream services directly. A sudden drop in incoming traffic is usually caused by something upstream, not your service.

## Step 3: Rule out upstream and downstream causes [#step-3]

<img
  title="distributed tracing in APM"
  alt="A screenshot depicting distributed tracing information on the APM summary page"
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_distributed-tracing-in-the-apm-summary-page.webp"
/>

Before concluding the problem lives in your service, check **Distributed tracing insights**. This section surfaces upstream and downstream entities showing correlated changes in response time, error rate, or throughput at the same time as your service.

**What to do:**
1. Scroll to **Distributed tracing insights** on the summary page.
2. Look for entities marked with a change indicator. Entities showing increased latency or error rate at the same time as your incident are likely related.
3. If a **downstream** service (one your code calls) shows degradation: click **View trace** to open a sample trace and identify exactly where time is being introduced. The problem may not be yours to fix — bring in the team that owns that service.
4. If an **upstream** service (one that calls yours) shows degradation: your service may be receiving malformed requests or bursts triggered by that caller.

If distributed tracing insights shows no correlated changes, the problem is isolated to your service. Continue to Step 4.

Learn more in the [distributed tracing insights docs](/docs/distributed-tracing/ui-data/related-trace-entity-signals/).

## Step 4: Check whether it's one host or all of them [#step-4]

<img
  title="Infrastructure summary page"
  alt="A screenshot depicting the infrastructure section of the apm summary page."
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_infrastructure-on-apm-summary-page.webp"
/>

Scroll to the **Infrastructure** section and compare response time, throughput, error rate, CPU, and memory across hosts.

| What you see | What it means | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| One host stands out | Isolated problem — bad deployment, resource constraint, or config drift on that host | Click the host row → check CPU/memory/disk; compare agent version and config against the rest of the fleet |
| All hosts show the same pattern | Shared cause — database, external API, or fleet-wide code change | Return to [Step 2](#step-2) and focus on the database or external segments |
| All hosts show errors but normal response time | A shared dependency (auth, feature flags, external API) is returning errors | Check **External services** for a call with a sudden increase in error rate |

## Step 5: Look for log patterns [#step-5]

<img
  title="logs in apm"
  alt="A screenshot showing logs in apm"
  src="/images/apm_screenshot-crop_logs-in-apm.webp"
/>

The **Logs** chart gives a summary view of your application logs through the [logs in context](/docs/logs/logs-context/get-started-logs-context/) feature.

**What to do:**
1. Select **Log patterns (top 10)** to surface groups of logs that share the same structure.
2. Look for a pattern that appeared at roughly the same time as the incident — one you don't recognize from normal operation. That's your lead.
3. Click **Logs** to open the full logs UI and filter by the time range of the incident for deeper analysis.

For more, see [Log patterns](/docs/logs/ui-data/find-unusual-logs-log-patterns/).

## Summary page not showing the cause? [#dead-end]

The APM summary page shows service-level aggregates. If the problem affects only a subset of users or a single endpoint, the signal may be diluted. Try these:

* **Transactions** → sort by **Most time consuming** or **Slowest average response time** to find a single degraded endpoint.
* **Errors inbox** → filter by time range to see individual error types and stack traces not visible in the aggregate error rate.
* **Service map** at **[one.newrelic.com > APM > your app > Service map](/docs/understand-dependencies/understand-system-dependencies/service-maps/service-maps/)** → visual view of which dependencies are unhealthy.
* **Query builder** → run `SELECT count(*) FROM TransactionError FACET error.message SINCE 1 hour ago` to see the most frequent error messages in the last hour.
